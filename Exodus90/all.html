A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And they came to Jericho; and as he was leaving Jericho with his disciples and a great multitude, Bartimae′us, a blind beggar, the son of Timae′us, was sitting by the roadside. And when he heard that it was Jesus of Nazareth, he began to cry out and say, “Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!” And many rebuked him, telling him to be silent; but he cried out all the more, “Son of David, have mercy on me!” And Jesus stopped and said, “Call him.” And they called the blind man, saying to him, “Take heart; rise, he is calling you.” And throwing off his mantle he sprang up and came to Jesus. Reflection

The last sign that Jesus works in St. John’s Gospel is the raising of Lazarus. In St. Mark’s Gospel, the last miracle that Jesus works before his triumphant entrance into Jerusalem is the curing of the blind man—a man who is the son of a Gentile (Timaeus) but who is named as a Hebrew, Bartimaeus. This last miracle unites the entire message of the Gospel up to this point: Christ has come to extend his redemption beyond Israel to all men. He comes to undo the curse of Adam and to transform us into the sons of God. He wants us to call out in prayer to him in the midst of our suffering and ask for his mercy. We call out when we pray, when we fast, and when we extend mercy to others. And in these activities, Christ comes to us, enters our experience, and transforms it, giving us a share in his divine life and redemptive work.

He gives us faith so that we can know God as he does. And having faith, we can follow him in hope, confident that he will supply for all we lack, enabling us to go where he is going and do what he is doing. Often We often hear Christians say that such and such a charitable act is the work of a Mother Teresa, or that intense prayer is what monks do. The implication is that what Christ did and the way he did it is only obtainable by a select few. Instead, St. Mark’s Gospel tells us: ask God to transform you into Christ. Deepen your experience of faith: hear God’s word! Strive to see his face! Do everything you can to make this gift a reality, even though nothing you can do will ever earn such a gift. Pray! Fast! Give alms! And have confidence that he will transform you. Get up and follow him, even to his Cross.

But as you follow him in faith and hope, it will become clear that there is yet a further and more perfect way that Christ wishes to transform you. We will consider the virtue of charity next week.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And when they drew near to Jerusalem, to Beth′phage and Bethany, at the Mount of Olives, he sent two of his disciples, and said to them, “Go into the village opposite you, and immediately as you enter it you will find a colt tied, on which no one has ever sat; untie it and bring it. Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Blessed is the kingdom of our father David that is coming! Hosanna in the highest!” And he entered Jerusalem, and went into the temple; and when he had looked round at everything, as it was already late, he went out to Bethany with the twelve.

Reflection

Palm Sunday is an intermediate day, partly anticipating the joy of Easter, partly anticipating the deep sorrows of the Cross. If the Transfiguration of Christ manifests to us the virtue of hope, Palm Sunday is a manifestation to us of divine charity, partially hidden, and partially revealed.

St. Paul writes that there are three virtues that endure into heaven because they pertain to God. These virtues are more divine than human, transforming us even now to enable us to participate in the divine-human life of Jesus Christ. Those three virtues are faith, hope, and love. St. Paul adds that the greatest of these is love. Love is the crown of the virtues, the most God-like activity that we can do. God alone perfectly loves himself, with the Father infinitely breathing forth the Spirit of Love upon his Son. All creatures are made God-like to the extent that they love as God loves. No creature can love the infinitely lovable God as much as he can and should be loved; they can only love God as much they are able, striving always to contemplate God with love. Perpetual, conscious contemplation of God belongs to eternal life. Only those purified beings in heaven are capable of that perfection of love.

But in this life, we must love consciously and unconsciously. A husband cannot think of his wife every second, but he must still be faithful to his vows at every moment of the day, even when he is not consciously thinking of his wife. So too for Christians in this life, we will need to sleep, focus on difficult tasks, and the rest, but at all times we must live such that nothing we do or think is more important to us than God. Further, everything we do needs to be ultimately—if not always consciously—ordered to God. Just as a married man must order all of his work toward loving and caring for his wife, so too must we order all we do to our loving relationship with God.

We are in an intermediate stage: at times our love for God is manifest and conscious, and at times it is hidden and unconscious. The grace God has poured into our hearts is hidden, but the more conscious acts of love we make, the more we strive to live in God’s presence, the more we will experience his grace flowing into our hearts. Although the perfection of the saints in heaven is not yet possible to us, we must nevertheless endeavor to emulate it. During this last week of Lent, it is especially good to be as consciously attentive to God and to make as many acts of love of him as possible. Every act of love will make our love for God increase.

On this Palm Sunday, make conscious acts of love toward God, rejoicing in his love and goodness, and praying to be transformed by his Paschal love.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

On the following day, when they came from Bethany, he was hungry. And seeing in the distance a fig tree in leaf, he went to see if he could find anything on it. When he came to it, he found nothing but leaves, for it was not the season for figs. And he said to it, “May no one ever eat fruit from you again.” And his disciples heard it. And when evening came they went out of the city.

Reflection

After triumphantly entering into Jerusalem, Jesus returns to Bethany to stay with Mary, Martha, and Lazarus. On the next morning he returns to Jerusalem, and on the way he curses the barren fig tree. Adam and Eve had clothed themselves in the leaves of the fig tree, but Jesus declares it is no longer the time for the fallen dispensation. He has come to replace the fig tree with the tree of life, the tree of the Cross which he will plant in the heart of every man.

The fig-tree was a sign of hiding from God, a sign of a mode of seeing the world that no longer was in loving communion with the Creator. Now, Christ comes to reveal the fullness of divine love, to restore a loving marital union to his people. Whereas the fig tree hides, Christ reveals. The fig tree represents a rebellion against God’s commandments, but Christ inscribes them in our hearts.

Benedict XVI sums up this vision of Christ’s love in Deus Caritas Est (#17):

In the gradual unfolding of this encounter, it is clearly revealed that love is not merely a sentiment. Sentiments come and go.… It is characteristic of mature love that it calls into play all man's potentialities; it engages the whole man. Contact with the visible manifestations of God's love can awaken within us a feeling of joy born of the experience of being loved. But this encounter also engages our will and our intellect. Acknowledgment of the living God is the one path towards love, and the “yes” of our will to his will unites our intellect, will and sentiments in the all- embracing act of love. But this process is always open-ended; love is never ‘finished’ and complete; throughout life, it changes and matures, and thus remains faithful to itself.… The love-story between God and man consists in the very fact that this communion of will increases in a communion of thought and sentiment, and thus our will and God's will increasingly coincide: God's will is no longer for me an alien will, something imposed on me from without by the commandments, but it is now my own will, based on the realization that God is in fact more deeply present to me than I am to myself. Then self- abandonment to God increases and God becomes our joy.

During Holy Week, rejoice in the love that Christ has for you. Invite him into your heart to purify it from all other attachments, that you may abandon yourself completely to a loving worship of the Bridegroom. May we be transformed.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And they came again to Jerusalem. … And one of the scribes came up and heard them disputing with one another, and seeing that he answered them well, asked him, “Which commandment is the first of all?” Jesus answered, “The first is, ‘Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one; and you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your mind, and with all your strength.’ The second is this, ‘You shall love your neighbor as yourself.’ There is no other commandment greater than these.” And the scribe said to him, “You are right, Teacher; you have truly said that he is one, and there is no other but he; and to love him with all the heart, and with all the understanding, and with all the strength, and to love one’s neighbor as oneself, is much more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices.” And when Jesus saw that he answered wisely, he said to him, “You are not far from the kingdom of God.” And after that no one dared to ask him any question. Reflection

On Tuesday of Holy week, Jesus visits the temple for the last time, and from within the temple he declares: greater than all burnt offerings and sacrifices is loving God with all your heart, mind, and strength. What makes Jesus’ coming sacrifice of infinite value is not merely the suffering and the blood, but rather, the intensity and perfection of his love for the Father and his love for all mankind. And this act of love is total: he gives himself entirely: body, blood, soul, and divinity. There is no more that he can give.

Christ invites us to reciprocate in love. He invites us to hasten to him with the entirety of our being: our heart, soul, mind, and strength. As St. Nicholas Cabasilas writes,

[Christ] is the resting place of human desires; he is the good of our thoughts. We are to love him alone. But that we may turn our love toward him at all times… there is no need of any special formulas for prayers, nor need to call upon him from any special place. There is no place in which he is not present; it is impossible for him not to be near us. For those who seek him He is actually closer than their very hearts.” (Life in Christ, Bk. VI.13)

Our love is not limited to the temple; nor do we have to express it through particular animal sacrifices. Rather, at all times, and in all places we are to live in such a way that all of our life is a loving gift of self to Christ. At times we may not be able to be actively attentive to his love, but we must always make this love the fundamental driving force of our lives.

If we do this, our very life is an expression of heavenly life. St. Nicholas continues:

All that is ours follows the will and moves where it is born by it.… If then, to imitate Christ and to live according to him is to live in Christ, this life is the effect of the will when it obeys God’s purposes.… What then may life be more fittingly called than love? For that which alone survives and does not allow the living to die when all things have been taken away is life—and such is love. When all things have passed away in the age to come love remains, and it alone suffices for life in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Life in Christ, Bk. VII.15)

Today, pray acts of love to God. In your prayer, will to love him with all your heart, all your soul, all your mind, and all your strength precisely because he alone is God. Give your whole self to God with love, for Christ has given his whole self to you with love.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

It was now two days before the Passover and the feast of Unleavened Bread. And the chief priests and the scribes were seeking how to arrest him by stealth, and kill him; for they said, “Not during the feast, lest there be a tumult of the people.” And while he was at Bethany in the house of Simon the leper, as he sat at table, a woman came with an alabaster jar of ointment of pure nard, very costly, and she broke the jar and poured it over his head. But there were some who said to themselves indignantly, “Why was the ointment thus wasted? She has done what she could; she has anointed my body beforehand for burying. Reflection

On this Wednesday of Holy Week, what we also call Spy Wednesday, we are reminded of the order of love: God, and God alone, is our first love. He has loved us completely and extravagantly, and we are to respond to him in like manner. The ointment of the woman was worth 300 denarii, almost a year’s worth of day wages. St. John’s Gospel tells us that it was Judas Iscariot who objected to this “waste” of the ointment when the money could have been given to the poor, “not that he cared for the poor but because he was a thief, and as he had the money box he used to take what was put into it” (John 12:6). Although comparing ancient currencies is difficult, when Judas agrees to betray Jesus for 30 pieces of silver, he is probably accepting no more than half the value of the perfume.

We cannot allow our love for God to be moderated under the pretext of love for our neighbor; when we do this, it will also undermine the love of our neighbor which we profess to be concerned with. As Benedict XVI writes: "Practical activity will always be insufficient, unless it visibly expresses a love for man, a love nourished by an encounter with Christ” (Deus Caritas Est, #34).

Our love for Christ is what enlivens our love for our neighbor. As Benedict XVI continues:

Faith, hope, and charity go together. Hope is practiced through the virtue of patience, which continues to do good even in the face of apparent failure, and through the virtue of humility, which accepts God's mystery and trusts him even at times of darkness. Faith tells us that God has given his Son for our sakes and gives us the victorious certainty that it is really true: God is love! It thus transforms our impatience and our doubts into the sure hope that God holds the world in his hands and that in spite of all darkness he ultimately triumphs in glory. Faith, which sees the love of God revealed in the pierced heart of Jesus on the Cross, gives rise to love. Love is the light—and in the end, the only light—that can always illuminate a world grown dim and give us the courage needed to keep living and working. Love is possible, and we are able to practice it because we are created in the image of God. To experience love and in this way to cause the light of God to enter into the world." (Deus Caritas Est, #39)

Today, renew the right ordering of love within yourself. We must love others because Christ loves us, and we love him. Do not be like the Pharisee who remains impassive even when Christ is present in his home; be transformed by charity! Do not be like Judas and let selfish greed masquerade as concern for others. Be overwhelmed by love for Jesus! Anoint him with your love, and allow him to anoint you with his love. Unite all of your work, all of your successes and failures to his loving sacrifice of his life. Let your love be joined to his, and let that love illuminate even the darkness of the tomb. Be transformed.

And when it was evening he came with the Twelve. Reflection

We must seek the food that truly satisfies us. Benedict XVI tells us, in Deus Caritas Est (#13):

Jesus anticipated his death and resurrection by giving his disciples, in the bread and wine, his very self, his body and blood as the new manna. The ancient world had dimly perceived that man's real food—what truly nourishes him as man—is ultimately the Logos, eternal wisdom: this same Logos now truly becomes food for us—as love. The Eucharist draws us into Jesus' act of self-oblation. More than just statically receiving the incarnate Logos, we enter into the very dynamic of his self-giving. The imagery of marriage between God and Israel is now realized in a way previously inconceivable: it had meant standing in God's presence, but now it becomes union with God through sharing in Jesus' self-gift, sharing in his body and blood. The sacramental “mysticism”, grounded in God's condescension towards us, operates at a radically different level and lifts us to far greater heights than anything that any human mystical elevation could ever accomplish.

The Eucharist is the bread of charity: it feeds and sustains our love for Christ. Each time we receive the Eucharist we should strive to receive Christ with more zeal, more longing, more attention than we have done previously, and in this way, our charity will increase. Christ gives himself to us in the Eucharist in the measure that we give ourselves to him. It is the moment when we surrender to God’s love in response to his incredible self-gift of love and life that he offers to us.

At this moment, we participate in the heavenly banquet; our life here and the life of the world to come meet. Jesus Christ is the substance of our eternal life; and our love, which we have received from him, is what lets us participate in that substance. All else fades in the presence of this reality.

Christians have been celebrating the Eucharistic feast for two thousand years. Allow the Lord to open the eyes of your heart to understand this life-changing mystery. Receive the love of God and let it transform you.

Reflection

St. Mark begins his account abruptly, announcing that this story will be a "gospel"—an euangelion. In the Roman Empire, an euangelion was a message declaring an imperial victory; the emperor had conquered, and his subjects could now rejoice in the peace and liberty that he had brought them.

But St. Mark's Gospel, unlike an imperial proclamation, begins by telling us that while there has been a great victory, we first need to remember what we need to be saved from. And John the Baptist is the man chosen for that vocation. John appears like a new mighty warrior of God, like Samson or the soldiers of ancient Israel, who were fed by wild honey in the wilderness (cf. Judges 14:8-9; I Samuel 14:24-26). But the war John the Baptist fights is one of repentance; he is here to remind all of Judea that they must radically repent. John's entire life—his intense fasting, his austere dress, his remote dwelling in the wilderness—all of this penance testifies to our radical need to repent. We all have sinned. We all have fallen into sins that have damaged our relationship with God, our relationship with each other, our relationship with ourselves, and our relationship with the world around us. John fasts to tame his own bodily desires; he dwells in the wilderness to withdraw from sinful creatures and strengthen his relationship with God; he robes himself in animal skins to live in simple poverty. Stripping himself of all worldly comforts, he shows the Israelites a complete alienation from all that they naturally seek and calls them to seek God and his holiness instead.

John the Baptist proclaims a double message: wash yourselves in the waters of repentance for your sins, but know that it is not enough. Begging for forgiveness is only the beginning. What we must yearn for is not merely to live a normal human life with less sin; God has placed a deeper desire within our hearts to walk with him and to be transformed into his perfect image and likeness. This is what we have lost due to sin. This must be the real motive for our repentance.

St. John also announces the good news of the One who will come after him, One greater than he who will transform us by baptizing us into Divine life. Our great penance is to prepare us to meet the One who comes after John. It is an expression not just of repentance for our sins but, even more, of our longing to be lifted up into union with God.

So let us fast intensely today. Let us withdraw quietly into our hearts, withdrawing especially from distracting, superficial relationships, and let us pray with John in repentance for our failings, but even more, that we may yearn for a transforming union with God. Let us receive our ashes, but may those ashes be a sign not just of sorrow for sin but also of our yearning for transforming union with God.

Reflection

When the Messiah comes to John the Baptist, he asks John to baptize him, to admit him into the ranks of repentant sinners. What an awesome and terrifying paradox. How can John baptize and instruct the Christ whom John was preparing by penance to welcome? How can he share his own life of penance with Christ?

As Jesus arises out of the water, John the Baptist sees the heavens open and "the Spirit descend as a dove from heaven and remain on him (John 1:32), and he hears the voice from heaven proclaiming Jesus as the beloved Son of God. John not only learns who Jesus is, he learns that the God whom he has served all his life is a Trinity: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. Further still, God the Son is present here to him, being baptized. This revelation must have been like an electric shock as John realized that this man before him was God the Son. What a profound mystical experience! John had shared in Jesus' own vision of the Holy Spirit, and in that moment, he also understood why Jesus wanted to be baptized.

In the Gospel of St. John the Evangelist, John the Baptist explains the reason by saying, "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29). St. Mark, however, prefers to show the meaning without spelling it out: immediately after baptism, Jesus is taken by the Spirit into the wilderness for forty days to be tempted by Satan. The message is the same in both Gospels: God has become man to take upon himself our sins and to make our penance and struggle his own, not because he owes penance, but because he wants to enter into our penance and thereby become the lamb who offers his penance as a sacrifice on our behalf. He wants to transform our repentance and fasting and other penitential acts into a means of transforming us into himself. If we share in Christ's suffering and reparation for sin, then our suffering and penance become signs of our closeness to him. And when we share that penance with Christ, we, like John the Baptist, will share in Christ's own relationship with his Father. We will hear the Father tell us that we are beloved in Christ, and we will receive, in Christ, the gift of the Anointing that makes him Christ the anointed. In other words, we will receive the Holy Spirit through our penitential union with Jesus.

Jesus shows us today that participating in the disciplines he took upon himself for forty days in the wilderness is the first step in allowing him to transform us. Let us follow Jesus into the desert of Lent, repenting with him. The wilderness of our penance is transformed into a garden of Eden (cf. Isaiah 41:17-20), another place of joy because God is with us. Let us joyfully undertake our penance, therefore, knowing that in doing so, we are clinging ever closer to our God Incarnate, Jesus Christ.

When Adam and Eve fell from grace in Eden, God gave them both the punishment of painful work: for Eve, God declared that she would conceive and bring forth children in toiling pain, and for Adam, God said that in toil and sweat he would bring forth bread from the earth (see Genesis 3:16-19). The Hebrew word is the same in both cases, iṣṣabon, and both times it implies not only physical discomfort and effort but also mental anguish and anxiety. It is a sign of the lack of harmony that now exists between man and the wider world: no longer are things like eating and bringing forth new life effortless. Instead, our life is a constant struggle, a fight for resources, success, recognition, and the rest. And it is doomed to failure. The last line of God's punishment reads, "In the [anxious] sweat of your face you shall eat bread till you return to the ground, for out of it you were taken; you are dust, and to dust you shall return." Even if he is successful in some venture, in the end he will still die. The author of Ecclesiastes takes this anxious toil as indicative of man's miserably vain condition: everything is pointless, because after a life of toil, we've earned only death.

When Christ emerges from his own victorious struggle with temptation, the first thing he offers to Simon, Andrew, James, and John is relief from vain toil. He frees them from pointless labor and begins their training for a work they can be confident will endure: the work of proclaiming the new kingdom of God and capturing the hearts of men for their King. Immediately after he calls these disciples, Jesus enters a synagogue on the sabbath, that blessed day of rest from toil, and casts out a demon—the root of man's perpetual restlessness.

This does not mean that Jesus brings us a life with no effort. Certainly not. But it does mean that he wants to transform our efforts into meaningful, enduring results. Whereas before, effort was always filled with a certain anxious fear because we knew there was a horizon that would reveal our work as a vain show, now our pains and anxieties and efforts can be free from captivity to the devil and free to serve an eternal King and his kingdom.

"Repent," Jesus cries, echoing John the Baptist. But the repentance is coupled with belief in the good news of his coming victory; the labor of penance and of life is no longer a pointless exercise whereby earthly existence is possibly made a little better. Now penance is a divine work, undertaken with Christ. Let us therefore gladly undertake the work of our repentance, offering up our labors and sacrifices for our own sanctification and the sanctification of those we love.

Reflection

Adam and Eve had fallen, and even their relationship to their own bodies was broken. Their bodies did what they did not want to do, and in the end, the body would do what they did not want the most: the body would die. Leprosy, a walking death, was a symbol of that rupture between the soul and body caused by sin, of the doomed destiny of each person. In leprosy, the flesh decayed and fell off so that his body seemed like a corpse even before he died. It was repulsive, and it recalled with unrelenting clarity the final end of all our struggles. It raised fear in everyone who saw it because it was the destiny of their bodies as well. The leper's cry may very well have been like the cry of St. Paul, "Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death" (Romans 7:24)?

And this dark destiny was the result of Adam's rejection of his purpose. His soul was not strong enough to resist the seductive lies of the devil and to simply trust God and bend his will to God's commands. God made Adam in his own image and established him in his friendship. He was to live in submission to God—trusting him, depending upon him, and obeying him. But Adam, and every sinner since Adam, has rejected that vocation, and so each of us finds ourselves unable to make our bodies and our wills always live and act as we would want. We have bodies of death, and not vessels of honor and grace.

But Christ came to restore this divine life in us. He wants to bring us, the walking dead, back to life and reestablish us in his friendship. But how? By his wonderful plan of love and sacrifice, foreshadowed by the sacrifice that Moses commanded for the cure of leprosy. For Moses ordered that to be cleansed, a leper must bring two birds to the priest. One bird would be sacrificed over running water. The living bird would be dipped into the blood of the dead bird and this blood the priest would then sprinkle over the leper seven times and pronounce him clean, and the living bird would be set free (see Leviticus 14:1-7). In order to save the human race, now walking in the leprosy of sin, Christ, in his great compassion, willingly sacrificed his life. His blood, through the waters of baptism, washes over us and divine life is restored to our souls.
Only then are we set free to fulfill God's glorious purpose for our lives.

Christ has come to restore us. To be with us. To cleanse us by the blood of his suffering and death on the cross offered to his Father to satisfy in his own flesh for our sins. Let us stir up our gratitude. Let us spend time today thanking the Father for his forgiveness, thanking the Son for his love and suffering. And let us pray that throughout this Lent, we will be ever more renewed in the divine life that he has, at such a great cost, restored to us.

Observe Sunday as a Little Easter. Lent is actually forty-six days. We speak of forty days in the desert with Christ because we do not count the six Sundays between Ash Wednesday and Easter as a part of Lent. Every Sunday celebrates the resurrection. For this reason, Sunday is not a day of fasting in the Church's tradition, and during this Lenten exercise, we have a mitigation of our disciplines.

The Church's liturgical life draws together an annual cycle of fasting and feasting. Lent, of course, focuses primarily on asceticism, but as we grow in discipline, we should be able to enter more deeply into true festivity and celebration. Without stopping our spiritual exercises, we enter into the joy of holy days and honor them through our prayer and, on the most solemn days, by lightening our disciplines.

Each Sunday is a little Easter on which we remember and celebrate the Lord's Day, the day of the Lord's Resurrection. Every Sunday, therefore, your fraternity is permitted to relax one discipline together to remind you of the glory of God and anticipation of the joy of Easter. Choose one discipline to relax as a fraternity to preserve fraternal unity and accountability. It is strongly advised that you do not relax technology-related disciplines.

  2. The Annunciation of the Lord on Saturday, March 25th

Reflection

Why does Jesus eat with sinners? Yesterday, Jesus revealed his divinity: he is the omnipotent creator of heaven and earth who can forgive sins. Today, we see his divine splendor surrounded by many sinners. He is God, but the people feasting with him are the poor, the sinners, the wretched. Why does God not dwell in a palace? Why is he not attended by kings, and served by the wealthiest, and the wisest, and the best?

In answer to the Pharisees, Jesus could have pointed out how even the very best that humanity could offer is infinitely beneath the glory of God. He could have laughed at how foolish it is for a Pharisee to think he is holy, for a king to think he is mighty, or for a scribe to think he is wise when in the company of omnipotence and omniscience. But he did not.

Instead, Jesus presents to them the fundamental point: why is he here at all? The question should be not who is God eating with, but why God is eating on earth? Or walking on earth? What could possibly explain the infinite, eternal Being walking the earth in a weak, human body?
And the answer Jesus gives is: I have come to heal the sick; to forgive sins, to restore what is broken. The reason God is on earth as a man is to act as the Divine physician, to call and spiritually heal mankind. Man is spiritually broken, alienated from his own being, from his fellow men, from his God. And God has come to heal that brokenness.

Moreover, he has come to heal this fatal wound of man by entering into his nature. St. Athanasius is clear on this point: if God wanted to merely forgive sins, he could do so by decree. But God wants to do more. He wants to be one of us—to share our humanity and teach us how to share his divinity—healing us from the inside out.

Of course, Jesus cannot make sin itself to be something that is not alienating from God. That is why he shows us how to resist temptations when he answers the devil in the desert. That is why he calls Levi away from his sinful practices. But once the sinner has responded with contrition and penance, as John the Baptist called all to do, then Jesus can heal the devastating effects of sin. He can come to the mess and brokenness created by our sins which we cannot fix, no matter how much we may want to, and he can fill that brokenness with his healing presence.

Spend time today reflecting on your own life. Where are the areas left broken by your sins? And then invite Jesus into those areas to transform them.

We are wedded to Christ. He cannot be taken from us, although we ourselves can abandon him. So let us turn today with all of our powers of attention and cleave to him. Let us invite him into our fasting so that he might break down whatever attachments keep us away from him. And let us affirm with joy that God becoming man has joined us to him forever.

Reflection

“Why are they doing what is not lawful on the sabbath?”

Throughout this week, Jesus has been revealing himself as the God-Man present in our pain, who transforms our pain into joy with his presence. But we need to be careful and not assume that we cannot feel pain, or sadness, or other “negative” emotions because as good Christians we should always be happy. Why are Jesus and his disciples plucking grain and eating it on the sabbath? Because they are hungry. They are poor. They are exhausted. Jesus himself really feels those things. Yes, he is God, but he is also man. And as man he finds himself tired and hungry on the day of rest.

The sabbath was instituted by God as a day when all mankind could rest from their toil and focus on the worship of the Lord. God-made-man entered into our fallen condition, and he and his followers were in need of food on the sabbath day like King David and the soldiers with him who were on a mission and could not stop to worship at the ark of the covenant (see 1 Samuel 21:1-7). David was worshipping God by fulfilling his will, and God, through the sign of the showbread, was present to him in his need and hunger.

As man, Jesus toils. As God, he gives rest. And he does this at one and the same moment. His divine rest enters into our anxious toil and becomes a real experience of his divine presence. This is not a contradiction. God’s presence is life-giving; and whenever we are receiving God’s life, we are entering into his rest. It is always lawful for God to feed and heal on the sabbath because the sabbath was made for man to be with God. Reflection

Last week we considered how Jesus knows the truth in two ways: he knows reality with his divine intellect, and he knows it with his human intellect. Similarly, Jesus has two wills: a divine will and a human will. This means, therefore, that Jesus loves us divinely and humanly. All humans also love in a two-fold way. We love by an act of the will so that we desire goodness for another in the way that we desire goodness for ourselves. For example, when a father loves his son, he wants what is best for his son, so much so that he is willing to sacrifice himself for his son’s good precisely because his love has made his son’s good his own good. This love of the will is done in light of the immortality of our souls; it transcends the limits of our merely bodily desires.

But there is another way we love. Love in this sense is in our emotions and is tied to our bodies: we get butterflies in the stomach, we flush, our heart beats faster. This emotional love is love too, and often there is a back and forth between the love of our wills and the love of our emotions: sometimes emotional love moves our wills to love, and sometimes our wills stir up our emotions.

When Jesus loves, he loves in a three-fold way. Pope Pius XII explains this:

The physical heart of Jesus Christ, united to the divine Person of the Word, beat with love and with the other emotions but these, joined to a human will full of divine charity and to the infinite love itself which the Son shares with the Father and the Holy Spirit, were in such complete unity and agreement that never among these three loves was there any contradiction or disharmony. . . . When Jesus loves us, he loves us as he has from all eternity as God. He also loves us with his grace-filled human will, and he loves us physically with his emotions. As St. Maximus of Turin writes, “in Christ our own flesh loves us.”
When Jesus comes to heal our pain, therefore, he does not come dispassionately. He arrives at the tombs and sees the man with an unclean spirit, crying out and bruising himself. And he feels deep compassion for him, and also for us, bound as we are by our sins and suffering. His divine love expresses itself in human will and in a physical heartbeat.

As we think about almsgiving this week, let us remember the deeply personal love that should motivate such gifts. Spend today meditating on Jesus’ threefold love, and rejoice that you are loved so well.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

Then he returned from the region of Tyre, and went through Sidon to the Sea of Galilee, through the region of the Decapolis. And they brought to him a man who was deaf and had an impediment in his speech; and they besought him to lay his hand upon him. And taking him aside from the multitude privately, he put his fingers into his ears, and he spat and touched his tongue; and looking up to heaven, he sighed, and said to him, “Ephphatha,” that is, “Be opened.” And his ears were opened, his tongue was released, and he spoke plainly. And he charged them to tell no one; but the more he charged them, the more zealously they proclaimed it. And they were astonished beyond measure, saying, “He has done all things well; he even makes the deaf hear and the dumb speak.”

Reflection

After healing the Syrophoenician woman’s daughter, Jesus comes to her native land, the land associated with Baal in the Old Testament and with Greek apostasy in the New Testament.
He finds there a man who has never heard the words of Moses; he has never encountered the revelation of God in any way. He is not just deaf and dumb physically, as he is also unaware of the revealed Word of God.

Jesus could have revealed himself secretly in the stillness of the man’s heart. Faith is indeed an interior reality as we have seen this week, but faith also measures our interior thoughts against an objective measurement. If faith were only an emotional, subjective disposition to God, there would be no need for external teaching, no need for a written book of Scripture or apostolic teachers. But God knows that in our brokenness we are too often willing to convince ourselves that our feelings are the inspiration of God; our misunderstandings and broken desires we may justify as signs of the divine will.

We need Jesus not only to join himself to us interiorly, but also to provide us with a sure norm that we can follow and measure ourselves against. For this reason, the teachings of faith are written in Scripture and set forth in fixed clear terms by Church councils and wise teachers. As the Catechism of the Catholic Church notes:

We do not believe in formulas, but in those realities they express, which faith allows us to touch. ‘The believer's act [of faith] does not terminate in the propositions, but in the realities [which they express].’ All the same, we do approach these realities with the help of formulations of the faith which permit us to express the faith and to hand it on, to celebrate it in community, to assimilate and live on it more and more. (CCC, #170)

This is why when Jesus heals the deaf man in the way he does. He reaches inside of him and enters into his affliction so as to plant the life of faith within his heart. At the same time, he opens his ears to be able to hear the external guides and teachings that will help his faith to grow, and he heals his tongue, making the man capable of expressing and embodying the interior life of faith he now receives.

Today, spend a few minutes giving thanks to God for the external teachers of the faith that he has put into your life, and pray for the grace to respond well to their teaching.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

In those days, when again a great crowd had gathered, and they had nothing to eat, he called his disciples to him, and said to them, “I have compassion on the crowd, because they have been with me now three days, and have nothing to eat; and if I send them away hungry to their homes, they will faint on the way; and some of them have come a long way.” And his disciples answered him, “How can one feed these men with bread here in the desert?” And he asked them, “How many loaves have you?” They said, “Seven.” And he commanded the crowd to sit down on the ground; and he took the seven loaves, and having given thanks he broke them and gave them to his disciples to set before the people; and they set them before the crowd. And they had a few small fish; and having blessed them, he commanded that these also should be set before them. And they ate, and were satisfied; and they took up the broken pieces left over, seven baskets full. And there were about four thousand people. And he sent them away; and immediately he got into the boat with his disciples, and went to the district of Dalmanu′tha. The Pharisees came and began to argue with him, seeking from him a sign from heaven, to test him. And he sighed deeply in his spirit, and said, “Why does this generation seek a sign? Truly, I say to you, no sign shall be given to this generation.” And he left them, and getting into the boat again he departed to the other side.

Reflection

Jesus works the miracle of the loaves and fish a second time, this time in the land of the Gentiles. In the land of the twelve tribes of Israel, he fed the multitudes with twelve baskets left over as a sign of his messianic restoration of the kingdom of Israel. Now he feeds the four thousand and has seven baskets left over. Seven is the number that recalls creation, for it was in seven days that God created the heavens and the earth. In feeding the Gentiles, Jesus is promising not only to restore Israel, but to restore all mankind to the glory for which it was destined at the beginning.

Letting us see creation with the eyes of faith is part of that restoration. When Adam laid his eyes upon the newly created world he knew first and foremost that each thing brought to him was a gift from God intended to help Adam express his inner relationship with God. Adam’s loving relationship with God was itself an experience of truth that opened his eyes to see in union with his beloved Lord.

Christ, in the days after restoring hearing to the pagan man, now restores the Gentiles to a communion with him—inviting them to eat with him and share in his life and vision, and inviting them into a new covenant of mutual faithfulness to each other.

Pope Francis writes:

Because faith-knowledge is linked to the covenant with a faithful God who enters into a relationship of love with man and speaks his word to him, the Bible presents it as a form of hearing … Knowledge linked to a word is always personal knowledge; it recognizes the voice of the one speaking, opens up to that person in freedom and follows him or her in obedience. Paul could thus speak of the ‘obedience of faith’ (cf. Romans 1:5; 16:26). Faith is also a knowledge bound to the passage of time, for words take time to be pronounced, and it is a knowledge assimilated only along a journey of discipleship. The experience of hearing can thus help to bring out more clearly the bond between knowledge and love. (Lumen Fidei, #29)

The Pharisees, lacking a loving relationship, are blind to the obvious signs that God is working in Jesus Christ. They ask him for a sign, and Jesus sighs deeply and refuses to give them another sign, knowing that they are unwilling to see and believe.

In our Lenten journey, Christ invites us to fast, pray and give alms, so that we can learn to deny ourselves and strengthen our spiritual communion with him. He also asks us to receive his sacred body given to us in Word and sacrament, so he can nourish our loving union with him. Let us hear him as Adam heard God speak in the garden. Today, pray for an increase in faith that you may render more loving obedience as you strive to better express your faith in your daily life.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

Now they had forgotten to bring bread; and they had only one loaf with them in the boat. And he cautioned them, saying, “Take heed, beware of the leaven of the Pharisees and the leaven of Herod.” And they discussed it with one another, saying, “We have no bread.” And being aware of it, Jesus said to them, “Why do you discuss the fact that you have no bread? Do you not yet perceive or understand? Are your hearts hardened? Having eyes do you not see, and having ears do you not hear? And do you not remember? When I broke the five loaves for the five thousand, how many baskets full of broken pieces did you take up?” They said to him, “Twelve.” “And the seven for the four thousand, how many baskets full of broken pieces did you take up?” And they said to him, “Seven.” And he said to them, “Do you not yet understand?”

Reflection

When we have an experience, we generally reflect on it. That’s what humans do. We may reflect more or less well, and we may or may not learn the right lessons, but experience always teaches us something when we think about what happens to us. When Christ became man, he chose to learn by experience. He knew all things from eternity in his divine mind, and his human mind received the fullness of that revelation from God; but he also still grew in wisdom by learning things experientially. When the hemorrhaging woman touched him, he asked who had touched him so he could experience physically seeing her whose faith he knew interiorly. So it should be no surprise that Jesus expects us to reflect upon our own experience of faith. And when we do that, we are engaging in theology: faith seeking further understanding.

Pope Francis writes:

Faith... draws us into itself, inviting us... better to know the object of our love. Christian theology is born of this desire. Clearly, theology… is part of the very process of faith, which seeks an ever deeper understanding of God’s self-disclosure culminating in Christ. It follows that theology is more than simply an effort of human reason to analyze and understand, along the lines of the experimental sciences. God cannot be reduced to an object. He is a subject who makes himself known and perceived in an interpersonal relationship. Right faith orients reason to open itself to the light which comes from God, so that reason, guided by love of the truth, can come to a deeper knowledge of God... [This] is a participation in God’s own knowledge of himself. It is not just our discourse about God, but first and foremost the acceptance and the pursuit of a deeper understanding of the word which God speaks to us, the word which God speaks about himself, for he is an eternal dialogue of communion, and he allows us to enter into this dialogue.... [We need] humility to be ‘touched’ by God, admitting [our] own limitations before the mystery, while striving to investigate the inexhaustible riches of this mystery. (Lumen Fidei, #36)

When we reflect on our experience of faith, we may do it badly. We may, like the Pharisees, come to rely more on our own thoughts and desires than on the foundations of faith, or we may, like the disciples in the boat, interpret our experiences in terms of merely earthly realities. Christ revealed his plan of divinizing redemption, and the disciples could only think about where to get more bread. So too, we can overshadow the light of faith by our earthly cares. During Lent, therefore, we try to think less about eating food and gaining money, and instead empty ourselves to receive spiritual gifts.

Spend time today reflecting on your experience of faith so far during this Lent. Ask the Holy Spirit to help you hear what Christ is trying to tell you.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And they came to Bethsa′ida. And some people brought to him a blind man, and begged him to touch him. And he took the blind man by the hand, and led him out of the village; and when he had spit on his eyes and laid his hands upon him, he asked him, “Do you see anything?” And he looked up and said, “I see men; but they look like trees, walking.” Then again he laid his hands upon his eyes; and he looked intently and was restored, and saw everything clearly. And he sent him away to his home, saying, “Do not even enter the village.”

Reflection

After returning to Galilee, Jesus gives sight to a blind, Jewish man. Here is a man who knows the revelation of the Old Covenant; he knows what God has done for his people. But he is a man who has lost his sight: he remembers what trees and men look like, but he has lost his vision. He does not see the point of his life now, or how all his suffering and toil are a part of God’s plan. He does not yet perceive God’s plan to redeem us and make us partakers of his divinity.

There are two stages to how the blind man is brought to see the face of Christ: first he sees imperfectly, as if he sees that Christ is not like the other men around. He sees “trees walking”; that is, he sees a man acting well beyond the capacity of any man. But he does not yet clearly see who Jesus is. Indeed, for Mark, we cannot fully see that until we are able to gaze upon Christ crucified. Only then can we fully know who he is. And so the blind man is brought through two healings: first to perceive the miracles and wonders of Jesus, and then, when he looks more intently, to see into the depths more clearly.

So too for us: we must look not only to the “highs” of our faith journey: the experiences of joy and confidence. We must accustom ourselves to look more intently to recognize Jesus in our struggles and sufferings. This happens when we allow Jesus to touch our hearts. As Pope Francis writes:

The light of love is born when our hearts are touched and we open ourselves to the interior presence of the beloved, who enables us to recognize his mystery. Thus we can understand why, together with hearing and seeing, Saint John can speak of faith as touch, as he says in his First Letter: ‘What we have heard, what we have seen with our eyes and touched with our hands, concerning the word of life’ (1 John 1:1). By his taking flesh and coming among us, Jesus has touched us, and through the sacraments he continues to touch us even today; transforming our hearts, he unceasingly enables us to acknowledge and acclaim him as the Son of God. In faith, we can touch him and receive the power of his grace. To touch him with our hearts: that is what it means to believe. (Lumen Fidei, #31)

A crowd brings the blind man to Jesus, but it seems none of them touch Jesus with personal faith, a faith that apprehends the mystery that he is the Son who reveals the Father. Jesus must lead the blind man away out of the village, and touching him, molds the man to himself, so that he may receive the faith needed to see and understand who Jesus is.

Today, on this Fifth Sunday of Lent ask Jesus to touch you in his Sacrament of Love, and to deepen your gaze of faith so that you may see ever more clearly who Jesus is and who he wants to transform you to be.

Reflection

We are not only called to share Christ’s vision of God and the world—we are called to make things happen. As Benedict XVI notes:

The Gospel is not merely a communication of things that can be known—it is one that makes things happen and is life-changing. The dark door of time, of the future, has been thrown open. The one who has hope lives differently; the one who hopes has been granted the gift of a new life. (Spe Salvi, #2)

The gift of faith is an encounter with the living God, and that encounter is life-changing. Pope Benedict continues:

Jesus, who himself died on the Cross, brought something totally different: an encounter with the Lord of all lords, an encounter with the living God and thus an encounter with a hope stronger than the sufferings of slavery, a hope which therefore transformed life and the world from within. (Spe Salvi, #4)

This encounter of faith needs to penetrate even into an encounter with Christ crucified. Peter, in today’s reading, sees Jesus as the Messiah, the promised, chosen Christ of God. But Peter will have to encounter Christ betrayed, killed, and risen, to encounter the Lord who by his own suffering and death, overcomes death. As Benedict XVI concludes:

The true shepherd is one who knows even the path that passes through the valley of death; One who walks with me even on the path of final solitude, where no one can accompany me, guiding me through: he himself has walked this path, he has descended into the kingdom of death, he has conquered death, and he has returned to accompany us now and to give us the certainty that, together with him, we can find a way through. The realization that there is One who even in death accompanies me, and with his ‘rod and his staff comforts me,’ so that ‘I fear no evil,’ this was the new ‘hope’ that arose over the life of believers. (Spe Salvi, #6)

Faith alone is not enough, although it is essential. Faith gives us an encounter with the living Christ and we begin to understand what our life is meant to be. But hope builds on this encounter. Not only are we invited to participate in Christ’s vision of the world, we are invited to have confident hope in his having overcome the wages of sin, which is death. Hope lets us make Christ’s conquering our own: with the help of his grace, we will safely pass through even the sufferings and terrors of our own deaths. The eternally satisfying goods that belong to Jesus will be shared with us, and so, making his good our own, we make his hope our own as well.

Today, pray in hope. In confidence ask Christ to give you the kingdom of God, even now, within your own heart. Ask him to let the eternal life that he has promised begin now in this earthly life.

And he said to them, “Truly, I say to you, there are some standing here who will not taste death before they see the kingdom of God come with power.” And after six days Jesus took with him Peter and James and John, and led them up a high mountain apart by themselves; and he was transfigured before them, and his garments became glistening, intensely white, as no fuller on earth could bleach them. And Peter said to Jesus, “Master, it is well that we are here; let us make three booths, one for you and one for Moses and one for Elijah.” For he did not know what to say, for they were exceedingly afraid. And a cloud overshadowed them, and a voice came out of the cloud, “This is my beloved Son; listen to him.” And suddenly looking around they no longer saw any one with them but Jesus only.

Reflection

Benedict XVI writes:

“[Our] great hope can only be God, who encompasses the whole of reality and who can bestow upon us what we, by ourselves, cannot attain. The fact that it comes to us as a gift is actually part of hope. God is the foundation of hope... the God who has a human face and who has loved us to the end.... His Kingdom is present wherever he is loved and wherever his love reaches us. His love alone gives us the possibility of soberly persevering day by day, without ceasing to be spurred on by hope, in a world which by its very nature is imperfect. His love is at the same time our guarantee of the existence of what we only vaguely sense and which nevertheless, in our deepest self, we await: a life that is ‘truly’ life. (Spe Salvi, #31)

We can make Christ’s confidence our own, precisely because we can rely on God’s love and power to give us divine goods. Hope, therefore, is two-fold: it lets us see the rewards and graces given to Christ as things that are also meant for us. Those divine gifts—the gift of knowing God, of being made his son, of acting with the strength of his mighty power—those gifts belong to us, even here on earth, inasmuch as we partake in Christ. And that is the second part of hope: we have confidence in claiming these divine gifts because we have them as gifts. If we did not realize this glory is ours in virtue of divine graciousness, then we would either not really understand how awesome these gifts are, or how lowly we are. But in either case, we would not be sharing in Christ’s confidence, because Christ both comprehends the abyss between God and man and utter confidence in his Father’s infinite love to overcome that abyss.

When we share in Christ’s own confidence, the divine goods of the Kingdom of God will be accessible in our present lives. Christ did not walk the earth in transfigured glory, but hid that reality within him, a reality present by his confident union with his Father in his human will, even as he prepared to be crucified. Christ’s transfiguration is a physical manifestation of the reality of our hope. Such hope is awe-inspiring because it opens up expectations that we had never before considered.

If we share in Christ’s confidence and grace, we will also share in the labors asked of him as he pursued eternal life. We may, like Peter, desire to bask in the confidence of hope, without enduring the struggles. But God is gentle, and he reminds us that he will be with us, like a pillar of cloud, to shade us in the burning heat of our struggles. Pray in hope today, asking God in his infinite mercy to sustain you through the remaining labors of Lent, and to fill you with grace of the forgiveness of sins and the hope of resurrected life.

St. Paul writes that there are three virtues that endure into heaven because they pertain to God. These virtues are more divine than human, transforming us even now to enable us to participate in the divine-human life of Jesus Christ. Those three virtues are faith, hope, and love. Just as a married man must order all of his work toward loving and caring for his wife, so too must we order all we do to our loving relationship with God.

We are in an intermediate stage: at times our love for God is manifest and conscious, and at times it is hidden and unconscious. The grace God has poured into our hearts is hidden, but the more conscious acts of love we make, the more we strive to live in God’s presence, the more we will experience his grace flowing into our hearts. Although the perfection of the saints in heaven is not yet possible to us, we must nevertheless endeavor to emulate it. And they came again to Jerusalem. … And one of the scribes came up and heard them disputing with one another, and seeing that he answered them well, asked him, “Which commandment is the first of all?” Jesus answered, “The first is, ‘Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one; and you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your mind, and with all your strength.’ The second is this, ‘You shall love your neighbor as yourself.’ There is no other commandment greater than these.” And the scribe said to him, “You are right, Teacher; you have truly said that he is one, and there is no other but he; and to love him with all the heart, and with all the understanding, and with all the strength, and to love one’s neighbor as oneself, is much more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices.” And when Jesus saw that he answered wisely, he said to him, “You are not far from the kingdom of God.” And after that no one dared to ask him any question. And he sat down opposite the treasury, and watched the multitude putting money into the treasury. Many rich people put in large sums. And a poor widow came, and put in two copper coins, which make a penny. And he called his disciples to him, and said to them, “Truly, I say to you, this poor widow has put in more than all those who are contributing to the treasury. For they all contributed out of their abundance; but she out of her poverty has put in everything she had, her whole living.”

Reflection

On Tuesday of Holy week, Jesus visits the temple for the last time, and from within the temple he declares: greater than all burnt offerings and sacrifices is loving God with all your heart, mind, and strength. What makes Jesus’ coming sacrifice of infinite value is not merely the suffering and the blood, but rather, the intensity and perfection of his love for the Father and his love for all mankind. And this act of love is total: he gives himself entirely: body, blood, soul, and divinity. There is no more that he can give.

Christ invites us to reciprocate in love. He invites us to hasten to him with the entirety of our being: our heart, soul, mind, and strength. As St. Nicholas Cabasilas writes,

[Christ] is the resting place of human desires; he is the good of our thoughts. We are to love him alone. But that we may turn our love toward him at all times… there is no need of any special formulas for prayers, nor need to call upon him from any special place. There is no place in which he is not present; it is impossible for him not to be near us. For those who seek him He is actually closer than their very hearts.” (Life in Christ, Bk. VI.13)

Our love is not limited to the temple; nor do we have to express it through particular animal sacrifices. Rather, at all times, and in all places we are to live in such a way that all of our life is a loving gift of self to Christ. At times we may not be able to be actively attentive to his love, but we must always make this love the fundamental driving force of our lives.

If we do this, our very life is an expression of heavenly life. St. Nicholas continues:

All that is ours follows the will and moves where it is born by it.… If then, to imitate Christ and to live according to him is to live in Christ, this life is the effect of the will when it obeys God’s purposes.… What then may life be more fittingly called than love? For that which alone survives and does not allow the living to die when all things have been taken away is life—and such is love. When all things have passed away in the age to come love remains, and it alone suffices for life in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Life in Christ, Bk. VII.15)

Today, pray acts of love to God. In your prayer, will to love him with all your heart, all your soul, all your mind, and all your strength precisely because he alone is God. Give your whole self to God with love, for Christ has given his whole self to you with love.

It was now two days before the Passover and the feast of Unleavened Bread. And the chief priests and the scribes were seeking how to arrest him by stealth, and kill him; for they said, “Not during the feast, lest there be a tumult of the people.” And while he was at Bethany in the house of Simon the leper, as he sat at table, a woman came with an alabaster jar of ointment of pure nard, very costly, and she broke the jar and poured it over his head. But there were some who said to themselves indignantly, “Why was the ointment thus wasted? Reflection

On this Wednesday of Holy Week, what we also call Spy Wednesday, we are reminded of the order of love: God, and God alone, is our first love. He has loved us completely and extravagantly, and we are to respond to him in like manner. The ointment of the woman was worth 300 denarii, almost a year’s worth of day wages. St. John’s Gospel tells us that it was Judas Iscariot who objected to this “waste” of the ointment when the money could have been given to the poor, “not that he cared for the poor but because he was a thief, and as he had the money box he used to take what was put into it” (John 12:6). Although comparing ancient currencies is difficult, when Judas agrees to betray Jesus for 30 pieces of silver, he is probably accepting no more than half the value of the perfume.

We cannot allow our love for God to be moderated under the pretext of love for our neighbor; when we do this, it will also undermine the love of our neighbor which we profess to be concerned with. As Benedict XVI writes: "Practical activity will always be insufficient, unless it visibly expresses a love for man, a love nourished by an encounter with Christ” (Deus Caritas Est, #34).

Our love for Christ is what enlivens our love for our neighbor. As Benedict XVI continues:

Faith, hope, and charity go together. Hope is practiced through the virtue of patience, which continues to do good even in the face of apparent failure, and through the virtue of humility, which accepts God's mystery and trusts him even at times of darkness. Faith tells us that God has given his Son for our sakes and gives us the victorious certainty that it is really true: God is love! It thus transforms our impatience and our doubts into the sure hope that God holds the world in his hands and that in spite of all darkness he ultimately triumphs in glory. Faith, which sees the love of God revealed in the pierced heart of Jesus on the Cross, gives rise to love. Love is the light—and in the end, the only light—that can always illuminate a world grown dim and give us the courage needed to keep living and working. Love is possible, and we are able to practice it because we are created in the image of God. To experience love and in this way to cause the light of God to enter into the world." (Deus Caritas Est, #39)

Today, renew the right ordering of love within yourself. We must love others because Christ loves us, and we love him. Do not be like the Pharisee who remains impassive even when Christ is present in his home; be transformed by charity! Do not be like Judas and let selfish greed masquerade as concern for others. Be overwhelmed by love for Jesus! Anoint him with your love, and allow him to anoint you with his love. Unite all of your work, all of your successes and failures to his loving sacrifice of his life. Let your love be joined to his, and let that love illuminate even the darkness of the tomb. Be transformed.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And when it was evening he came with the Twelve. And as they were at table eating, Jesus said, “Truly, I say to you, one of you will betray me, one who is eating with me.” They began to be sorrowful, and to say to him one after another, “Is it I?” He said to them, “It is one of the Twelve, one who is dipping bread into the dish with me. It would have been better for that man if he had not been born.” And as they were eating, he took bread, and blessed, and broke it, and gave it to them, and said, “Take; this is my body.” And he took a cup, and when he had given thanks he gave it to them, and they all drank of it. Truly, I say to you, I shall not drink again of the fruit of the vine until that day when I drink it new in the kingdom of God.” And when they had sung a hymn, they went out to the Mount of Olives.

Reflection

We must seek the food that truly satisfies us. Benedict XVI tells us, in Deus Caritas Est (#13):

Jesus anticipated his death and resurrection by giving his disciples, in the bread and wine, his very self, his body and blood as the new manna. The ancient world had dimly perceived that man's real food—what truly nourishes him as man—is ultimately the Logos, eternal wisdom: this same Logos now truly becomes food for us—as love. The Eucharist draws us into Jesus' act of self-oblation. More than just statically receiving the incarnate Logos, we enter into the very dynamic of his self-giving. The imagery of marriage between God and Israel is now realized in a way previously inconceivable: it had meant standing in God's presence, but now it becomes union with God through sharing in Jesus' self-gift, sharing in his body and blood. The sacramental “mysticism”, grounded in God's condescension towards us, operates at a radically different level and lifts us to far greater heights than anything that any human mystical elevation could ever accomplish.

The Eucharist is the bread of charity: it feeds and sustains our love for Christ. Each time we receive the Eucharist we should strive to receive Christ with more zeal, more longing, more attention than we have done previously, and in this way, our charity will increase. Christ gives himself to us in the Eucharist in the measure that we give ourselves to him. It is the moment when we surrender to God’s love in response to his incredible self-gift of love and life that he offers to us.

At this moment, we participate in the heavenly banquet; our life here and the life of the world to come meet. Jesus Christ is the substance of our eternal life; and our love, which we have received from him, is what lets us participate in that substance. All else fades in the presence of this reality.

Christians have been celebrating the Eucharistic feast for two thousand years. Allow the Lord to open the eyes of your heart to understand this life-changing mystery. Receive the love of God and let it transform you.

Reflection

Today, love has revealed itself. Benedict XVI writes, once again in Deus Caritas Est (#12):

Jesus’ death on the Cross is the culmination by which God gives himself in order to raise man up and save him. This is love in its most radical form. By contemplating the pierced side of Christ we can understand that “God is love” (1 John 4:8). It is there that this truth can be contemplated. It is from there that our definition of love must begin. In this contemplation the Christian discovers the path along which his life and love must move.

In Mark’s Gospel, the only person who confesses Jesus to be the Son of God is the centurion who sees Jesus breathing his last. It is this moment that completes Peter’s first confession that Jesus is the Christ. It is the second step in the gift of faith. Men knew there was something special about Jesus, but only on the Cross is the fullness of his divinity revealed. What is St. Mark saying?

Faith for its fullest expression requires love. While we might laugh at the lover who sees wondrous beauty in his beloved, it is perhaps most true that only the lover really sees his beloved as she really is. All the rest of us are not looking intently enough to see her dignity and worth. Jesus reveals himself in love; his last act is to breathe his last, that is, to give forth his Spirit, his love.

The Spirit revealed in faith at his baptism, the Spirit that accompanied him in hope at his transfiguration is now may manifest in love. In his most extreme agony, Jesus cries out to the Father who had proclaimed him at the beginning of his ministry. The Trinity revealed at his baptism in prophetic signs is now made manifest for all mankind upon the Cross.

Today, let us contemplate Christ crucified and see in him the fullest presence of divinizing love. Be transformed.

The mind may be transported by the Spirit to an eternal ocean of light, falling into ecstasy, transfigured with great joy. We come to the tomb today, and wait in loving silence, offering whatever gifts we are able to bring. And Christ will transform those meager gifts into an astonishing, ecstatic experience of divine love drawing us ever upward.

The earliest manuscripts of Mark’s Gospel end with this verse anticipating the vision of the risen Lord. St. Mark has led us through the transforming, divinizing journey of following Christ, and he leaves us waiting to encounter the risen Lord in the light of Easter morning.

Let us wait. Let us love. Let us be transformed.
