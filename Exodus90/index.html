A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

Now John’s disciples and the Pharisees were fasting; and people came and said to him, “Why do John’s disciples and the disciples of the Pharisees fast, but your disciples do not fast?” And Jesus said to them, “Can the wedding guests fast while the bridegroom is with them? As long as they have the bridegroom with them, they cannot fast. The days will come, when the bridegroom is taken away from them, and then they will fast in that day. No one sews a piece of unshrunk cloth on an old garment; if he does, the patch tears away from it, the new from the old, and a worse tear is made. And no one puts new wine into old wineskins; if he does, the wine will burst the skins, and the wine is lost, and so are the skins; but new wine is for fresh skins.”

Reflection

Why don’t your disciples fast? This seems like an odd question to us in the midst of our Lenten fast. Very clearly, the disciples of Jesus do fast, and at times exhaustingly so! It is all the more surprising to hear Jesus say that not only are his disciples not fasting, they cannot do so. But he promises that the time will come when they will fast.

What are we to make of this? First, Jesus is using fasting as a further way to reveal himself. He is not only God become Son of man who has entered into our humanity, he is also our Bridegroom. He does not leave us broken and sad, but has come to fill us with joy as at a wedding celebration. The fast of John the Baptist and the Pharisees stressed their sinfulness, their waiting for the coming of the Savior who would reunite God and man. Christians need not participate in this fast. That would be putting new wine into old wine skins. Christians are not longing for the coming of a Redeemer; instead, Christians are proclaiming that their Beloved is here with them, and his presence fills them with joy.

When Christians fast, therefore, we are always joyfully aware of the presence of our Redeemer. For this reason, we must “anoint your head and wash your face” (Matthew 6:17) and be content even when fasting. Our penance is no longer a sign of God’s absence, but it is a sign that we are journeying with our Bridegroom. What joy!

And yet, the Bridegroom is not fully present to us. This is not because he is far away, but rather, because our love is weak. Our Bridegroom is present in our souls through our love, and when our love is weak, it is as if he were far away, even though he is in reality more present to us than we are to ourselves. Therefore, we fast so we may grow in love, so that the God-Man may ever more be present in our souls and we may experience more fully the happiness of his presence.

We are wedded to Christ. He cannot be taken from us, although we ourselves can abandon him. So let us turn today with all of our powers of attention and cleave to him. Let us invite him into our fasting so that he might break down whatever attachments keep us away from him. And let us affirm with joy that God becoming man has joined us to him forever.

Week 1 Action Items

  1. Face temptations in prayer.
  2. Detach through asceticism.
  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others.

  1. Face temptations in prayer. The devil finds ways to encourage us to watch television, movies, or televised sports while at work, alone at home, or while waiting in line to distract us from doing the Lord’s will. Exercise freedom from these mind-numbing temptations. Stay attentive to God in prayer, the place where you can look these temptations in the face and address this with God.

    If you want to see real change in yourself, you need to spend undistracted time talking with and relying on God daily, and that starts today. For more on the importance of prayer and an explanation of silent, contemplative prayer, see Fidelity to Prayer from the Field Guide. If making a holy hour is a new practice for you, check out the Holy Hour Guide.

  2. Detach through asceticism. Through this Lenten exercise, God is giving you the opportunity to detach and be present to him and others. Use your mobile devices only for essential communications so that you can be present to those you love. Set your phone out of sight and embrace your freedom.

    This detachment cannot remain just a “no.” It needs to become a “yes” of love to God as we offer him these little sacrifices. We can also do so specifically for the intention of others in prayer. Abstaining from meat and fasting offers you opportunities of self-offering all day long. Accept the grace to say “no” to food and “yes” to a sacrifice for others. (For more on asceticism and self-offering see The Gift of Asceticism in the Field Guide.)

  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others. Penance aims at making things right. It acknowledges that we have sinned and need to sacrifice to make up for what we have done, giving up our attachments and getting rightly ordered to God. The best way to achieve the aim of penance is to go to Confession. God will not only give us the grace of forgiveness of our sins, but will also give us the grace that we need to embrace conversion, enter more deeply into penance, and live different.

    We also need to reconcile with others, repairing any damage that we have done in our relationships. As we set out on our journey, consider praying together with your family, and ask each person for forgiveness for the ways you may have hurt them.
