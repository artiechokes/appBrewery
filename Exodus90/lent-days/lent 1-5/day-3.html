Each day, it's important to take your bearings. Where are you today? In this Lenten exercise, in the scriptures, in your daily life, and in your very self? Spend just a minute or two getting your bearings for the day.

Reflection

When Adam and Eve fell from grace in Eden, God gave them both the punishment of painful work: for Eve, God declared that she would conceive and bring forth children in toiling pain, and for Adam, God said that in toil and sweat he would bring forth bread from the earth (see Genesis 3:16-19). The Hebrew word is the same in both cases, iṣṣabon, and both times it implies not only physical discomfort and effort but also mental anguish and anxiety. It is a sign of the lack of harmony that now exists between man and the wider world: no longer are things like eating and bringing forth new life effortless. Instead, our life is a constant struggle, a fight for resources, success, recognition, and the rest. And it is doomed to failure. The last line of God's punishment reads, "In the [anxious] sweat of your face you shall eat bread till you return to the ground, for out of it you were taken; you are dust, and to dust you shall return." Man's existence is a relentless battle with all aspects of life, and in the end, we will return to the dust we were made from. Why is man so anxious? No matter what he does, he will die. Even if he is successful in some venture, in the end he will still die. The author of Ecclesiastes takes this anxious toil as indicative of man's miserably vain condition: everything is pointless, because after a life of toil, we've earned only death.

When Christ emerges from his own victorious struggle with temptation, the first thing he offers to Simon, Andrew, James, and John is relief from vain toil. He frees them from pointless labor and begins their training for a work they can be confident will endure: the work of proclaiming the new kingdom of God and capturing the hearts of men for their King. Immediately after he calls these disciples, Jesus enters a synagogue on the sabbath, that blessed day of rest from toil, and casts out a demon—the root of man's perpetual restlessness.

This does not mean that Jesus brings us a life with no effort. Certainly not. But it does mean that he wants to transform our efforts into meaningful, enduring results. Whereas before, effort was always filled with a certain anxious fear because we knew there was a horizon that would reveal our work as a vain show, now our pains and anxieties and efforts can be free from captivity to the devil and free to serve an eternal King and his kingdom.

"Repent," Jesus cries, echoing John the Baptist. But the repentance is coupled with belief in the good news of his coming victory; the labor of penance and of life is no longer a pointless exercise whereby earthly existence is possibly made a little better. Now penance is a divine work, undertaken with Christ. Let us therefore gladly undertake the work of our repentance, offering up our labors and sacrifices for our own sanctification and the sanctification of those we love.

Today's Action Item

Embrace Asceticism. Lent is a time of prayer and penance. Fridays in Lent are even more intense days when everyone is called to abstain from meat, and we focus our attention on the Passion of Christ. This exercise should help you to make the most of Lent by embracing asceticism, the practice of penance, and self-denial. The combination of prayer, asceticism, and fraternity leads to inner conversion through external acts to provide you with a framework for growth. Each discipline that you embrace this Lent will provide you with an offering to God for your good and for your family. The suffering of saying "no" to deserts and sweets may be difficult, but if you see that your "no" is really a "yes" offered to the Lord, then your mindset will change. This comes through uniting your sacrifice to the cross and prayerfully offering the sacrifice to the Lord for your specific intention.

This Lenten exercise contains a specific list of ascetic disciplines. As you review the list, you may notice that the disciplines touch most aspects of daily life. Some may be easy for you, while others will be more challenging. If some disciplines seem unnecessary because you don't struggle with them, commit to doing them anyway. If some seem too hard for you to handle, commit to those too. In both situations, the Lord has gifts he wants to give you through them as a whole by creating a spirit of docility and humility. The practice of asceticism is challenging. It is a sacrifice and a cross. Take up your cross this Lent and offer your whole life as a worthy sacrifice to the Lord, holding nothing back.

The asceticism of these 40-plus days will prune us of our attachments and help us to focus on what is most important. It will provide us with a means of growing in inner strength by doing difficult things for the Lord. The exercises will provide us a means of making reparation for our sins, helping us to realize that our material attachments cannot satisfy us. In our dependence upon them, we act as if they could make us happy, but there is no amount of material pleasure that could ever bring us peace. We must turn to what can truly satisfy us in loving God and others. Our culture acts as if suffering should be avoided at all costs, but in making a sacrifice, we deliberately suffer for what is most important (as a soldier who suffers for his country). Of course, there is always a need for prudence. If there is something in your life that makes taking up one of the disciplines reckless or imprudent, don't take it up. Let your fraternity know of any disciplines you aren't able to practice so that you can live these forty days in the light, and your brothers can best hold you accountable.
