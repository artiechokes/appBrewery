A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

While he was still speaking, there came from the ruler’s house some who said, “Your daughter is dead. Why trouble the Teacher any further?” But ignoring what they said, Jesus said to the ruler of the synagogue, “Do not fear, only believe.” And he allowed no one to follow him except Peter and James and John the brother of James. When they came to the house of the ruler of the synagogue, he saw a tumult, and people weeping and wailing loudly. And when he had entered, he said to them, “Why do you make a tumult and weep? The child is not dead but sleeping.” And they laughed at him. But he put them all outside, and took the child’s father and mother and those who were with him, and went in where the child was. Taking her by the hand he said to her, “Talitha cumi”; which means, “Little girl, I say to you, arise.” And immediately the girl got up and walked; for she was twelve years old. And immediately they were overcome with amazement. And he strictly charged them that no one should know this, and told them to give her something to eat.

Reflection

The crowds laughed at Jesus because they did not believe in his generosity. We may imagine that our generosity will be respected, appreciated, and praised. But that is not always the case. Almsgiving can spark jealousy, accusations, suspicions, and fears, either among those who witness our giving, or even among those who benefit from it. Our fallen human nature with its mean, ungrateful, and base inclinations can turn against a benefactor even in moments when we should be grateful.

So, we must be ready to suffer when we give alms. Sometimes the suffering will come from the pain of parting with some of our goods. St. Basil noted long ago that the moment we have a possession, we form an intense attachment to it almost as if it were a part of us. The first pain of almsgiving, therefore, may well be just the parting with the possession. At other times, the almsgiving itself may be an occasion of ridicule as we share the Gospel, or are seen associating with a disreputable person. Our Lord’s acts of kindness to Samaritans and sinners were often occasions for criticism.

Our almsgiving may also be the gift of ourselves, our willingness to lay down our life or to suffer in our flesh for the sake of saving our neighbor. We must be ready and willing to lay down our life if it will save someone from eternal damnation. And there are many other ways whereby we may sacrifice ourselves in works of mercy.
The Second Vatican Council teaches:

“The Lord Jesus implied a certain likeness between the union of the divine Persons, and the unity of God's sons in truth and charity. This likeness reveals that man, who is the only creature on earth which God willed for itself, cannot fully find himself except through a sincere gift of himself.” (Gaudium et Spes, #24)

In the end, all of our almsgiving is to be a sincere gift of self. That is what gives it its value. If we give sacrificially, it reflects the proper meaning of the gift and helps further our union with our Lord.

Today, ask our Lord to show you how to give alms as an expression of your sincere gift of self.

Week 3 Action Items

  1. Make good use of time.
  2. Discipline and train your body and soul.
  3. Avoid grumbling and encourage others.

1. Make good use of time. If we are to guard our time of prayer, this will require staying focused on what needs to be done and avoiding distractions. When you have work that needs to be done on the computer, get it done. Complete the task at hand, then get off the computer. Hide your mobile device if that would help. The fundamentals of freedom are simple.

Stepping back from distractions is a gift. Use well the time that to you once was “lost and now has been found” (see Luke 15:32). Get outside your room and visit with your neighbor, your cousin, or your wife. Relish your time of prayer and sit with your daily Scripture reading and reflection, being nourished by them.

2. Discipline and train your body and soul. Your time of exercise can help you enter into asceticism. Try to maintain a regular schedule of exercise three days a week. The training and discipline of the body is an image of the interior battle as well.

Just as we must sacrifice regularly to get in shape, so we must commit to regular spiritual exercises. Fasting helps us to discipline and strengthen our will, putting the needs of the spirit before the body. Lean into Lenten fasting and abstinence with Christ. Even as we build discipline, he is the one who, through this fasting and penance, is making you a new man.

3. Avoid grumbling and encourage others. St. Benedict warned his monks that grumbling would undo all of their spiritual progress. As we get further along, we will become more tempted to grumble at those snacks we see but can’t have, and our sacrifices may tempt us to become impatient. Try to stay focused on your why and offer your sacrifice to God in love.

It also helps to acknowledge God’s grace regularly. At the end of every night, we are called to pause and examine our day with the Lord. Don’t forget to give God thanks for the many graces of the day—even the graces to see where you fell short and the desire to do better the next day. If we keep the right attitude, we can help keep others focused too. Try to offer encouragement to your family and fraternity to stay focused on Lent. If you avoid grumbling and stay rooted in humility and love, you will be a good witness for them.
