Reflection

Our Lord gave himself to all mankind. We are more limited in our capacity for self-giving, and what is more, a general desire to do something good for humanity may lead us to neglect the people right in front of us. That’s what is so marvelous about the way that Jesus gives himself to us. As Pope Pius XII notes:

The knowledge and love of our Divine Redeemer, of which we were the object from the first moment of His Incarnation, exceed all the human intellect can hope to grasp. For hardly was He conceived in the womb of the Mother of God, when [through his own vision of God] all the members of His Mystical Body were continually and unceasingly present to Him, and He embraced them with His redeeming love. O marvelous condescension of divine love for us! O inestimable dispensation of boundless charity! In the crib, on the Cross, in the unending glory of the Father, Christ has all the members of the Church present before Him and united to Him in a much clearer and more loving manner than that of a mother who clasps her child to her breast, or than that with which a man knows and loves himself. (Mystici Corporis, #75)

Jesus, in his human nature, was given the grace to know God as God exists in himself. Of course, this vision did not extend to the entirety of the infinite divine essence (no created intellect can do that), but Jesus, in his human intellect did have a direct perception of God, and, through that perception, a miraculous perception of each of us. Jesus loves all mankind, but he loves all by loving each particularly, not as some abstraction or generality that does not take into account our individuality.

As we discern how to imitate the expansiveness of Jesus’ almsgiving, we must keep in mind the particular way that Jesus gives. The more we are attentive to a particular human’s needs, to the individuality of the person involved, the more we are giving the way Christ gives. Certainly, the more people we can help the better, but it is not the larger numbers that matters as much as our love that is attentive to the particular individual and his needs.

Today, think of at least one person that you can give alms to in this manner, imitating the way Jesus loves. Then ask yourself whether you can extend that kind of love to at least two more people as well. Push yourself as far as you can to give as personally as possible to as many as possible.

Week 3 Action Items

  1. Make good use of time.
  2. Discipline and train your body and soul.
  3. Avoid grumbling and encourage others.

1. Make good use of time. If we are to guard our time of prayer, this will require staying focused on what needs to be done and avoiding distractions. When you have work that needs to be done on the computer, get it done. Complete the task at hand, then get off the computer. Hide your mobile device if that would help. The fundamentals of freedom are simple.

Stepping back from distractions is a gift. Use well the time that to you once was “lost and now has been found” (see Luke 15:32). Get outside your room and visit with your neighbor, your cousin, or your wife. Relish your time of prayer and sit with your daily Scripture reading and reflection, being nourished by them.

2. Discipline and train your body and soul. Your time of exercise can help you enter into asceticism. Try to maintain a regular schedule of exercise three days a week. The training and discipline of the body is an image of the interior battle as well.

Just as we must sacrifice regularly to get in shape, so we must commit to regular spiritual exercises. Fasting helps us to discipline and strengthen our will, putting the needs of the spirit before the body. Lean into Lenten fasting and abstinence with Christ. Even as we build discipline, he is the one who, through this fasting and penance, is making you a new man.

3. Avoid grumbling and encourage others. St. Benedict warned his monks that grumbling would undo all of their spiritual progress. As we get further along, we will become more tempted to grumble at those snacks we see but can’t have, and our sacrifices may tempt us to become impatient. Try to stay focused on your why and offer your sacrifice to God in love.

It also helps to acknowledge God’s grace regularly. At the end of every night, we are called to pause and examine our day with the Lord. Don’t forget to give God thanks for the many graces of the day—even the graces to see where you fell short and the desire to do better the next day. If we keep the right attitude, we can help keep others focused too. Try to offer encouragement to your family and fraternity to stay focused on Lent. If you avoid grumbling and stay rooted in humility and love, you will be a good witness for them.
