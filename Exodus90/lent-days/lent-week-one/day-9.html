A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

One sabbath he was going through the grainfields; and as they made their way his disciples began to pluck ears of grain. And the Pharisees said to him, “Look, why are they doing what is not lawful on the sabbath?” And he said to them, “Have you never read what David did, when he was in need and was hungry, he and those who were with him: how he entered the house of God, when Abi′athar was high priest, and ate the bread of the Presence, which it is not lawful for any but the priests to eat, and also gave it to those who were with him?”
And he said to them, “The sabbath was made for man, not man for the sabbath; so the Son of man is lord even of the sabbath.” Again he entered the synagogue, and a man was there who had a withered hand. And they watched him, to see whether he would heal him on the sabbath, so that they might accuse him. And he said to the man who had the withered hand, “Come here.” And he said to them, “Is it lawful on the sabbath to do good or to do harm, to save life or to kill?” But they were silent. And he looked around at them with anger, grieved at their hardness of heart, and said to the man, “Stretch out your hand.” He stretched it out, and his hand was restored. The Pharisees went out, and immediately held counsel with the Hero′dians against him, how to destroy him.

Reflection

“Why are they doing what is not lawful on the sabbath?”

Throughout this week, Jesus has been revealing himself as the God-Man present in our pain, who transforms our pain into joy with his presence. But we need to be careful and not assume that we cannot feel pain, or sadness, or other “negative” emotions because as good Christians we should always be happy. Why are Jesus and his disciples plucking grain and eating it on the sabbath? Because they are hungry. They are poor. They are exhausted. Jesus himself really feels those things. Yes, he is God, but he is also man. And as man he finds himself tired and hungry on the day of rest.

The sabbath was instituted by God as a day when all mankind could rest from their toil and focus on the worship of the Lord. God-made-man entered into our fallen condition, and he and his followers were in need of food on the sabbath day like King David and the soldiers with him who were on a mission and could not stop to worship at the ark of the covenant (see 1 Samuel 21:1-7). David was worshipping God by fulfilling his will, and God, through the sign of the showbread, was present to him in his need and hunger.

As man, Jesus toils. As God, he gives rest. And he does this at one and the same moment. His divine rest enters into our anxious toil and becomes a real experience of his divine presence. This is not a contradiction. God’s presence is life-giving; and whenever we are receiving God’s life, we are entering into his rest. It is always lawful for God to feed and heal on the sabbath because the sabbath was made for man to be with God. We can experience both the toil of our God-given duties and God’s rest in that toil because both are present simultaneously in Jesus.

In fasting we also find both toil and rest. As we fast we may feel hungry, weak, stressed, tired, overwhelmed, and we may come face-to-face with our own sinfulness. This is toil. But God fills our toil with his restorative love. Even as we fast, we find healing. We keep praying. We repent. And when we repent, God comes to fill us with his loving-kindness. He is there to remind us that he was hungry too, and that he has transformed the experience of hunger to be an encounter with divine mercy.

So in your time of prayer today, present to Christ your withered hand, the brokenness you are discovering in your penance. And ask him to give you his rest as you continue to toil.

Week 1 Action Items

  1. Face temptations in prayer.
  2. Detach through asceticism.
  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others.

  1. Face temptations in prayer. The devil finds ways to encourage us to watch television, movies, or televised sports while at work, alone at home, or while waiting in line to distract us from doing the Lord’s will. Exercise freedom from these mind-numbing temptations. Stay attentive to God in prayer, the place where you can look these temptations in the face and address this with God.

    If you want to see real change in yourself, you need to spend undistracted time talking with and relying on God daily, and that starts today. For more on the importance of prayer and an explanation of silent, contemplative prayer, see Fidelity to Prayer from the Field Guide. If making a holy hour is a new practice for you, check out the Holy Hour Guide.

  2. Detach through asceticism. Through this Lenten exercise, God is giving you the opportunity to detach and be present to him and others. Use your mobile devices only for essential communications so that you can be present to those you love. Set your phone out of sight and embrace your freedom.

    This detachment cannot remain just a “no.” It needs to become a “yes” of love to God as we offer him these little sacrifices. We can also do so specifically for the intention of others in prayer. Abstaining from meat and fasting offers you opportunities of self-offering all day long. Accept the grace to say “no” to food and “yes” to a sacrifice for others. (For more on asceticism and self-offering see The Gift of Asceticism in the Field Guide.)

  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others. Penance aims at making things right. It acknowledges that we have sinned and need to sacrifice to make up for what we have done, giving up our attachments and getting rightly ordered to God. The best way to achieve the aim of penance is to go to Confession. God will not only give us the grace of forgiveness of our sins, but will also give us the grace that we need to embrace conversion, enter more deeply into penance, and live different.

    We also need to reconcile with others, repairing any damage that we have done in our relationships. As we set out on our journey, consider praying together with your family, and ask each person for forgiveness for the ways you may have hurt them.
