A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

He went out again beside the sea; and all the crowd gathered about him, and he taught them. And as he passed on, he saw Levi the son of Alphaeus sitting at the tax office, and he said to him, “Follow me.” And he rose and followed him. And as he sat at table in his house, many tax collectors and sinners were sitting with Jesus and his disciples; for there were many who followed him. And the scribes of the Pharisees, when they saw that he was eating with sinners and tax collectors, said to his disciples, “Why does he eat with tax collectors and sinners?” And when Jesus heard it, he said to them, “Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick; I came not to call the righteous, but sinners.”

Reflection

Why does Jesus eat with sinners? Yesterday, Jesus revealed his divinity: he is the omnipotent creator of heaven and earth who can forgive sins. Today, we see his divine splendor surrounded by many sinners. He is God, but the people feasting with him are the poor, the sinners, the wretched. Why does God not dwell in a palace? Why is he not attended by kings, and served by the wealthiest, and the wisest, and the best?

In answer to the Pharisees, Jesus could have pointed out how even the very best that humanity could offer is infinitely beneath the glory of God. He could have laughed at how foolish it is for a Pharisee to think he is holy, for a king to think he is mighty, or for a scribe to think he is wise when in the company of omnipotence and omniscience. But he did not.

Instead, Jesus presents to them the fundamental point: why is he here at all? The question should be not who is God eating with, but why God is eating on earth? Or walking on earth? What could possibly explain the infinite, eternal Being walking the earth in a weak, human body? And the answer Jesus gives is: I have come to heal the sick; to forgive sins, to restore what is broken. The reason God is on earth as a man is to act as the Divine physician, to call and spiritually heal mankind. Man is spiritually broken, alienated from his own being, from his fellow men, from his God. And God has come to heal that brokenness.

Moreover, he has come to heal this fatal wound of man by entering into his nature. St. Athanasius is clear on this point: if God wanted to merely forgive sins, he could do so by decree. But God wants to do more. He wants to be one of us—to share our humanity and teach us how to share his divinity—healing us from the inside out.

Of course, Jesus cannot make sin itself to be something that is not alienating from God. That is why he shows us how to resist temptations when he answers the devil in the desert. That is why he calls Levi away from his sinful practices. But once the sinner has responded with contrition and penance, as John the Baptist called all to do, then Jesus can heal the devastating effects of sin. He can come to the mess and brokenness created by our sins which we cannot fix, no matter how much we may want to, and he can fill that brokenness with his healing presence.

Spend time today reflecting on your own life. Where are the areas left broken by your sins? And then invite Jesus into those areas to transform them.

Week 1 Action Items

  1. Face temptations in prayer.
  2. Detatch through asceticism.
  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others.

  1. Face temptations in prayer. The devil finds ways to encourage us to watch television, movies, or televised sports while at work, alone at home, or while waiting in line to distract us from doing the Lord’s will. Exercise freedom from these mind-numbing temptations. Stay attentive to God in prayer, the place where you can look these temptations in the face and address this with God.

    If you want to see real change in yourself, you need to spend undistracted time talking with and relying on God daily, and that starts today. For more on the importance of prayer and an explanation of silent, contemplative prayer, see Fidelity to Prayer from the Field Guide. If making a holy hour is a new practice for you, check out the Holy Hour Guide.

  2. Detach through asceticism. Through this Lenten exercise, God is giving you the opportunity to detach and be present to him and others. Use your mobile devices only for essential communications so that you can be present to those you love. Set your phone out of sight and embrace your freedom.

    This detachment cannot remain just a “no.” It needs to become a “yes” of love to God as we offer him these little sacrifices. We can also do so specifically for the intention of others in prayer. Abstaining from meat and fasting offers you opportunities of self-offering all day long. Accept the grace to say “no” to food and “yes” to a sacrifice for others. (For more on asceticism and self-offering see The Gift of Asceticism in the Field Guide.)

  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others. Penance aims at making things right. It acknowledges that we have sinned and need to sacrifice to make up for what we have done, giving up our attachments and getting rightly ordered to God. The best way to achieve the aim of penance is to go to Confession. God will not only give us the grace of forgiveness of our sins, but will also give us the grace that we need to embrace conversion, enter more deeply into penance, and live different.

    We also need to reconcile with others, repairing any damage that we have done in our relationships. As we set out on our journey, consider praying together with your family, and ask each person for forgiveness for the ways you may have hurt them.
