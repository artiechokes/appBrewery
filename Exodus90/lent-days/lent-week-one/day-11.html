Reflection

Who then is Jesus? Who is the man who experiences the effects of sin, without sinning, and what is more, who transforms his sufferings into redemptive moments? He must be a singular man, a person capable of suffering and redeeming in one and the same moment. This is the classic theological formulation that the Christian tradition has developed to express the mystery of Christ: he is one Person, a single unitary subject, but one who is fully God and fully man. When we ask who is acting, the answer is always, God the Word. However, the fact that God the Word is the subject does not mean he is not fully human. He acts in everything he does, in everything he suffers as both God and man. As God he is redeeming our human experience at the very same moment, he is suffering the experience in his human nature. There are not two different subjects cooperating together, but rather, one subject acting both divinely and humanly.

St. Maximos the Confessor explains this using the image of a fiery sword: the sword will both cut and burn in one and the same motion, having two different principles of action. So too with Christ: when he acts or suffers, there will always be both a fully divine action and a fully human action. To claim that Christ is only human, or only God, or partially both, is to obscure the profound miracle of the Holy Spirit and to deny the only path for the redemption of mankind. There is no salvation except through the truth of who Jesus Christ is.

We must, then, bow down and worship Jesus Christ, true God and true man. His picture is God’s picture, his touch, truly a divine touch. We worship God in a body. His human nature came to us in time, but it will forever continue united to the second Person of the Holy Trinity. This is an extraordinarily profound mystery, a truth that transcends the limits of our mind.

The Catechism quotes a prayer that the Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom uses to express this mystery: "O only-begotten Son and Word of God, immortal being, you who deigned for our salvation to become incarnate of the holy Mother of God and ever-virgin Mary, you who without change became man and were crucified, O Christ our God, you who by your death have crushed death, you who are one of the Holy Trinity, glorified with the Father and the Holy Spirit, save us!" (CCC, 469).

Let us worship Jesus, true God and true man, who opens to us the way of our Lenten journey.

Week 1 Action Items

  1. Face temptations in prayer.
  2. Detach through asceticism.
  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others.

  1. Face temptations in prayer. The devil finds ways to encourage us to watch television, movies, or televised sports while at work, alone at home, or while waiting in line to distract us from doing the Lord’s will. Exercise freedom from these mind-numbing temptations. Stay attentive to God in prayer, the place where you can look these temptations in the face and address this with God.

    If you want to see real change in yourself, you need to spend undistracted time talking with and relying on God daily, and that starts today. For more on the importance of prayer and an explanation of silent, contemplative prayer, see Fidelity to Prayer from the Field Guide. If making a holy hour is a new practice for you, check out the Holy Hour Guide.

  2. Detach through asceticism. Through this Lenten exercise, God is giving you the opportunity to detach and be present to him and others. Use your mobile devices only for essential communications so that you can be present to those you love. Set your phone out of sight and embrace your freedom.

    This detachment cannot remain just a “no.” It needs to become a “yes” of love to God as we offer him these little sacrifices. We can also do so specifically for the intention of others in prayer. Abstaining from meat and fasting offers you opportunities of self-offering all day long. Accept the grace to say “no” to food and “yes” to a sacrifice for others. (For more on asceticism and self-offering see The Gift of Asceticism in the Field Guide.)

  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others. Penance aims at making things right. It acknowledges that we have sinned and need to sacrifice to make up for what we have done, giving up our attachments and getting rightly ordered to God. The best way to achieve the aim of penance is to go to Confession. God will not only give us the grace of forgiveness of our sins, but will also give us the grace that we need to embrace conversion, enter more deeply into penance, and live different.

    We also need to reconcile with others, repairing any damage that we have done in our relationships. As we set out on our journey, consider praying together with your family, and ask each person for forgiveness for the ways you may have hurt them.
