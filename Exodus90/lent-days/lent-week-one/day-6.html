DAILY READING & REFLECTION
DAY 6

God Forgives Sin

Each day, it's important to take your bearings. Where are you today? In this Lenten exercise, in the scriptures, in your daily life, and in your very self? Spend just a minute or two getting your bearings for the day.

Let's briefly examine where we are in this Lenten exercise and then begin each day with a prayer for transformation--for ourselves, our fraternity brothers, and all Exodus Men.

You are traveling with Christ through Galilee. We have heard the call of Christ, and we have followed him, leaving worldly pleasures behind us. Every step of the way moves us further away from the stability of our previous life. As we travel through the countryside with Christ, the terrain is difficult, and we may miss some of our past pleasures, but there is a mysterious joy amidst the hardships. The source of that joy is in the person of Jesus Christ. He has called us to leave behind our normal life and to discover who he is. But this is no easy task. There is a mystery about Jesus, but he invites us to continue to ask questions. As we seek the answers, we will also come to see that the more we know about who he is, the more we will know about who he wants to transform us to be.

Let’s continue to separate ourselves from the things that are not Christ’s. He has big things in store for us and desires to purify us ever more. Let us be transformed!

Pray the Lord blesses you and your fraternity with a foundation for real personal change during this season of Lent.

Pray for the grace of perseverance for all Exodus Men, just as they are praying for you.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And many were gathered together, so that there was no longer room for them, not even about the door; and [Jesus] was preaching the word to them. And they came, bringing to him a paralytic carried by four men. And when they could not get near him because of the crowd, they removed the roof above him; and when they had made an opening, they let down the pallet on which the paralytic lay. And when Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, “My son, your sins are forgiven.” Now some of the scribes were sitting there, questioning in their hearts, “Why does this man speak thus? It is blasphemy! Who can forgive sins but God alone?” And immediately Jesus, perceiving in his spirit that they thus questioned within themselves, said to them, “Why do you question thus in your hearts? Which is easier, to say to the paralytic, ‘Your sins are forgiven,’ or to say, ‘Rise, take up your pallet and walk’?

But that you may know that the Son of man has authority on earth to forgive sins”—he said to the paralytic—“I say to you, rise, take up your pallet and go home.”

And he rose, and immediately took up the pallet and went out before them all; so that they were all amazed and glorified God, saying, “We never saw anything like this!”

Reflection

Jesus suddenly presents himself in Galilee with his message of a new kingdom that will deliver us from the effects of Adam’s and of our own transgressions. This message is revolutionary, never before heard in all its force and grandeur. It is a message more experienced than understood; the crowds were amazed as they saw Jesus cast out demons, but not because they comprehended his new teaching. It was the experience of his absolute authority not limited by any kind of natural law that led people to know that this was a new kind of rabbi.

Beginning in chapter two, Mark leads us through a series of questions that people ask about Jesus. In asking these questions ourselves, and in hearing Jesus’ responses, we are drawn ever more deeply to know who this new teacher is. Today, Jesus offers forgiveness of sins, so that we too may question in our hearts, “Who can forgive sins but God alone?” Jesus is truly God. The God whom David cried out to for forgiveness in Psalm 51 is the very one who now walks on earth. Only God can forgive sins, and therefore God incarnate can save us from our sins. No mere creature can pardon an offense against the infinite goodness of God. Sin at its core is an act of ingratitude and malice, an attack against the very Source of our being. But God is always able to draw forth something from nothing. As the catechism teaches: “Since God could create everything out of nothing, he can also, through the Holy Spirit, give spiritual life to sinners by creating a pure heart in them” (CCC, #298). He reveals his almighty power in the act of forgiving our sins. It is the same almighty power that creates everything out of nothing, the same power with which he restores health to lifeless limbs, and the same power that creates light out of darkness.

We do not often think of the immense rupture that our sins create between God and us. Nor do we think of the limitless love that induces God to bridge that unbridgeable divide. Today, spend some time contemplating that great breach and ponder with gratitude that the good God, whom we have so offended, wants to draw us close to him and heal us. God has become the Son of Man. We have never seen anything like this. Let us pray that we may be overwhelmed by such beauty and goodness, such magnificent grace.

Week 1 Action Item

  1. Face temptations in prayer. The devil finds ways to encourage us to watch television, movies, or televised sports while at work, alone at home, or while waiting in line to distract us from doing the Lord’s will. Exercise freedom from these mind-numbing temptations. Stay attentive to God in prayer, the place where you can look these temptations in the face and address this with God.

    If you want to see real change in yourself, you need to spend undistracted time talking with and relying on God daily, and that starts today. For more on the importance of prayer and an explanation of silent, contemplative prayer, see Fidelity to Prayer from the Field Guide. If making a holy hour is a new practice for you, check out the Holy Hour Guide.

  2. Detach through asceticism. Through this Lenten exercise, God is giving you the opportunity to detach and be present to him and others. Use your mobile devices only for essential communications so that you can be present to those you love. Set your phone out of sight and embrace your freedom.

    This detachment cannot remain just a “no.” It needs to become a “yes” of love to God as we offer him these little sacrifices. We can also do so specifically for the intention of others in prayer. Abstaining from meat and fasting offers you opportunities of self-offering all day long. Accept the grace to say “no” to food and “yes” to a sacrifice for others. (For more on asceticism and self-offering see The Gift of Asceticism in the Field Guide.)

  3. Seek Reconciliation with God and others. Penance aims at making things right. It acknowledges that we have sinned and need to sacrifice to make up for what we have done, giving up our attachments and getting rightly ordered to God. The best way to achieve the aim of penance is to go to Confession. God will not only give us the grace of forgiveness of our sins, but will also give us the grace that we need to embrace conversion, enter more deeply into penance, and live different.

    We also need to reconcile with others, repairing any damage that we have done in our relationships. As we set out on our journey, consider praying together with your family, and ask each person for forgiveness for the ways you may have hurt them.
