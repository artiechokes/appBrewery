But in the midst of the struggle, we are invited to enter the sacred silence that is Christ’s own prayer. He wants us to share in his own longing for and communion with the Father. Let us be transformed!

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

And he said, “The kingdom of God is as if a man should scatter seed upon the ground, and should sleep and rise night and day, and the seed should sprout and grow, he knows not how. The earth produces of itself, first the blade, then the ear, then the full grain in the ear. But when the grain is ripe, at once he puts in the sickle, because the harvest has come.”

Reflection

The second degree of prayer is mediative. Using the word degree here should not imply that vocal prayer is left behind; rather in our prayer we may go back and forth between the various degrees, as did Jesus himself. We see Jesus praying vocally in the synagogue, and then we see him leading us into meditative prayer through his parables. And we are told that he went into the wilderness, where he was immersed in prayer and the angels ministered to him (Mark 1:13,35), which we can assume refers to contemplative prayer. We too can move back and forth between these degrees, and sometimes the degrees interpenetrate each other so that in our conversing with God we use both vocal and meditative prayer. Each psalm of scripture is the fruit of meditation upon the word of God, which can then lead us into vocal prayers and inspire further meditation.

So profound is the experience of prayer, that in a way, we can see prayer as an experience of the kingdom of God. It brings us before God’s presence and transforms us into citizens of that kingdom. God’s word, present on the page, is illuminated by meditation and carried into our heart. We are called to meditate on it day and night (Psalms. 1:2), turning it over in our minds, ruminating upon it, and allowing it to imprint itself upon our hearts. As we repeat the words to ourselves, and reflect upon their meaning, the light of the Holy Spirit enables us to understand what God wants to reveal to us.

In meditation we can start with the smallest seed, a short verse of scripture, and as we turn it over in our minds, the meaning becomes clearer. What before was an obscure phrase now can take root in our hearts and can stretch into our daily lives so that we can see now how God wants us to act in a particular situation.

Today, therefore, take a short reading from scripture, either today’s selection or some other, and focus on just a short verse or phrase. Repeat it several times to yourself, and sit quietly reflecting upon its deeper meaning, asking God to help you understand what he is saying to you. Meditate on it day and night, letting it sink deeply into your heart, and it will grow into you in ways you cannot explain. In this way, the seed of God will grow and ripen, and we will have a harvest to return to him, the Sower of the seed.

Week 2 Action Items

  1. Imitate Christ’s love for the Father.
  2. Give up in order to give.
  3. Be a well set anchor.


1. Imitate Christ’s love for the Father. In our prayer, fasting, and giving we are seeking to be like Christ. We can speak about imitating him, but this is more than copying. When we enter our holy hour, we enter into Christ’s own prayer and become interiorly conformed to him, joined to his own love for the Father in the Holy Spirit. In our sacrifices, we are not just giving things up to be tough. We are learning to love like Christ, putting the Father before all else. Be both the one who offers the sacrifice for others and the sacrifice itself.

Everything we give up is ordered toward relationship with God. We make sacrifices to spend time with those who are important to us. Get up earlier, give up your lunch break, do what it takes to make time for God. Stay focused on the goal of union with God in all that we do this Lent.

2. Give up in order to give. Giving alms is a pillar of Lent for a reason. Nothing material in this world can completely satisfy a man. God gives us good things not to please us but to use for the good of others. Lent is a season of increased almsgiving, of greater attention to those in need. Make sacrifices for God and he will satisfy you. If you sacrifice unnecessary spending, you will be able to give more to others.

3. Be a well set anchor. The Christian life is made up of prayer, asceticism, and fraternity. If you live prayer and asceticism, but not fraternity, you’re doing something, but you’re not living the fullness of the Christian life. Lean in and build a bond with your brothers.

A key part of fraternity is being a good anchor. Stay honest with your anchor on how you are doing every day. Call him on to be honest and regular in his check-ins with you as well. Be a dependable, well set anchor.
