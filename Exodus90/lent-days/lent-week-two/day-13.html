The God-Man

Each day, it's important to take your bearings. Where are you today? In this Lenten exercise, in the scriptures, in your daily life, and in your very self? Spend just a minute or two getting your bearings for the day.

Let's briefly examine where we are in this Lenten exercise and then begin each day with a prayer for transformation--for ourselves, our fraternity brothers, and all Exodus Men.

You are in the wilderness. We are now two weeks into our journey and we are surrounded by the wilderness of Galilee. Like the disciples, we are likely reaching the point when our initial burst of enthusiasm is fading, and the daily weight of self-denial is wearing us out. But in the midst of the struggle, we are invited to renew ourselves by entering into Christ’s prayer and making it our own.

Jesus has revealed to us the mystery of who he is, and who he wants us to be. He has led us to detach ourselves from things that give us comfort so as to better cling to him. Now he invites us to go deeper, to enter the sacred silence that is his own prayer—to share in his own longing for our sanctification and a share in his communion with the Father—and this prayer will radically transform us. Come, brothers, let us be transformed!

Pray the Lord blesses you and your fraternity with a foundation for real personal change during this season of Lent.

Pray for the grace of perseverance for all Exodus Men, just as they are praying for you.

A Reading from the Gospel of Mark

Again he began to teach beside the sea. And a very large crowd gathered about him, so that he got into a boat and sat in it on the sea; and the whole crowd was beside the sea on the land. And he taught them many things in parables, and in his teaching he said to them: “Listen! A sower went out to sow. And as he sowed, some seed fell along the path, and the birds came and devoured it. Other seed fell on rocky ground, where it had not much soil, and immediately it sprang up, since it had no depth of soil; and when the sun rose it was scorched, and since it had no root it withered away. Other seed fell among thorns and the thorns grew up and choked it, and it yielded no grain. And other seeds fell into good soil and brought forth grain, growing up and increasing and yielding thirtyfold and sixtyfold and a hundredfold.” And he said, “He who has ears to hear, let him hear.”

Reflection

Jesus both sows the seed, is the seed, and receives the seed. This is the marvelous mystery of Jesus Christ, true God and true man. As God, he reveals himself to mankind. And as God the Word he is the word sent from the Father into the hearts of all believers. He is God’s own revelation of himself, and he himself is the fullness of God’s revelation. And as man, Jesus must receive the gift of revelation. This last point takes a little to grasp. God, in his divine nature, has a divine mind. In his divine mind he knows himself and all that he is going to do. But in addition to his divine mind, the Second Person of the Trinity assumed a human mind when he assumed a human nature. As the Catechism states, “This human soul that the Son of God assumed is endowed with a true human knowledge. As such, this knowledge could not in itself be unlimited: it was exercised in the historical conditions of his existence in space and time. This is why the Son of God could, when he became man, “increase in wisdom and in stature, and in favor with God and man," and would even have to inquire for himself about what one in the human condition can learn only from experience” (CCC, 472). But the human mind of Christ was not cut off from his divine mind by a wall of separation. As the Catechism explains, This truly human knowledge of God's Son expressed the divine life of his person. "The human nature of God's Son, not by itself but by its union with the Word, knew and showed forth in itself everything that pertains to God." Such is first of all the case with the intimate and immediate knowledge that the Son of God made man has of his Father. The Son in his human knowledge also showed the divine penetration he had into the secret thoughts of human hearts. By its union to the divine wisdom in the person of the Word incarnate, Christ enjoyed in his human knowledge the fullness of understanding of the eternal plans he had come to reveal. (CCC, 473-474) Jesus’ human mind, however, only shared in the divine knowledge through the gift of revelation. The human mind of its own cannot know the Father, or the Father’s eternal plans, or the secret thoughts of other hearts. All this knowledge only comes as a gift of revelation. Therefore, Jesus, in his divine nature reveals and is God’s Word, but in his human nature he receives this gift of revelation. He receives this revelation not for his own sake, but to share it with us. Today spend time in prayer asking Jesus to share his word with you. Ask him to help you hear it and let it take root in your mind just as it took root in his. He came so that we could share in his knowledge of God. Ask him for that gift.

Week 2 Action Items

1. Imitate Christ’s love for the Father. In our prayer, fasting, and giving we are seeking to be like Christ. We can speak about imitating him, but this is more than copying. When we enter our holy hour, we enter into Christ’s own prayer and become interiorly conformed to him, joined to his own love for the Father in the Holy Spirit. In our sacrifices, we are not just giving things up to be tough. We are learning to love like Christ, putting the Father before all else. Be both the one who offers the sacrifice for others and the sacrifice itself.

Everything we give up is ordered toward relationship with God. We make sacrifices to spend time with those who are important to us. Get up earlier, give up your lunch break, do what it takes to make time for God. Stay focused on the goal of union with God in all that we do this Lent.

2. Give up in order to give. Giving alms is a pillar of Lent for a reason. Nothing material in this world can completely satisfy a man. God gives us good things not to please us but to use for the good of others. Lent is a season of increased almsgiving, of greater attention to those in need. Make sacrifices for God and he will satisfy you. If you sacrifice unnecessary spending, you will be able to give more to others.

3. Be a well set anchor. The Christian life is made up of prayer, asceticism, and fraternity. If you live prayer and asceticism, but not fraternity, you’re doing something, but you’re not living the fullness of the Christian life. Lean in and build a bond with your brothers.

A key part of fraternity is being a good anchor. Stay honest with your anchor on how you are doing every day. Call him on to be honest and regular in his check-ins with you as well. Be a dependable, well set anchor.
